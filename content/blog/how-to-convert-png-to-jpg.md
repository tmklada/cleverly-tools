## PNG vs. JPG — What's the Difference?

Before converting, it helps to understand why these two formats exist and when each one makes sense.

### PNG (Portable Network Graphics)
- **Lossless compression** — No image quality is lost when saving or resaving
- **Supports transparency** — PNG allows transparent backgrounds (perfect for logos, icons)
- **Larger file sizes** — Higher quality means bigger files
- **Best for:** Logos, icons, screenshots, graphics with text, images that need transparency

### JPG (JPEG — Joint Photographic Experts Group)
- **Lossy compression** — Some image data is discarded to reduce file size
- **No transparency** — JPG always has a solid background
- **Smaller file sizes** — Often 3–10x smaller than an equivalent PNG
- **Best for:** Photos, social media images, web backgrounds, any image where file size matters

## Why Convert PNG to JPG?

Here are the most common reasons to make the switch:

- **Email attachments** — PNG files can be several MB; the same image as JPG might be under 500KB
- **Social media uploads** — Instagram, Facebook, and Twitter prefer JPG and often auto-convert anyway
- **Website speed** — Smaller JPG images load faster, improving your site's performance
- **Storage space** — Converting a folder of PNG screenshots to JPG can free up gigabytes
- **Platform compatibility** — Some older apps and systems handle JPG better than PNG
- **Printing** — Print labs often prefer JPG for photos

## What Quality Setting Should I Use?

JPG quality is measured from 1 to 100 (or a percentage). Here's a practical guide:

| Quality Setting | File Size | Visual Quality | Best For |
|---|---|---|---|
| 100% | Very large | Perfect | Archival, print-ready files |
| 80–90% | Medium | Excellent | Web images, social media |
| 60–75% | Small | Good | Email attachments, thumbnails |
| Below 50% | Very small | Noticeably degraded | Only if size is critical |

For most uses — websites, social media, email — 80–85% quality is the ideal balance. Files are dramatically smaller than PNG with no visible quality difference on screen.

## Will I Lose Quality Converting PNG to JPG?

Technically yes, but practically often no. Here's the nuance:

- **Photos:** A photo converted from PNG to JPG at 80%+ quality looks identical on screen. You'd need to zoom in significantly to spot any difference.
- **Graphics with text or sharp edges:** JPG compression can create slight blurriness around sharp edges and text — this is visible. For these, keep PNG or use a high quality setting (90%+).
- **Transparent areas:** PNG transparency becomes a white (or colored) background in JPG. If your image needs transparency, do not convert to JPG — stay with PNG or use WebP.
