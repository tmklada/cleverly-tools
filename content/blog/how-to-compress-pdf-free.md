## Why Are PDFs So Large in the First Place?

There are several reasons a PDF can be larger than expected:

- **Embedded fonts** — fonts are stored inside the file
- **High-resolution images** — photos taken on modern cameras are huge
- **Scanned pages** — each page is essentially a full image
- **Metadata and annotations** — comments, bookmarks, and form data add weight
- **Duplicate resources** — some PDF creators are inefficient with how they store data

Compression removes or reduces all of these while keeping the document readable.

## When Should You Compress a PDF?

Here are the most common reasons to reduce PDF size:

- **Emailing documents** — most email providers limit attachments to 10–25MB
- **Uploading to websites or portals** — job applications, government forms, school submissions
- **Sharing via WhatsApp or Telegram** — messaging apps have file size limits
- **Storing on your phone** — smaller files use less storage
- **Website uploads** — faster loading for users who download your PDFs online

## Does Compression Hurt the Quality?

For most documents, the answer is no — or at least not in any way you'd notice. Here's why:

- **Text and vector graphics** compress without any quality loss
- **Images** are where compression makes a tradeoff. At medium compression, image quality is reduced slightly — usually only visible if you zoom in very close
- **At high compression**, images may look noticeably softer if you have large, detailed photos

If quality matters most, use Low or Medium compression. If file size is the priority, use High compression.
