## What Makes a Scientific Calculator Different?

A basic calculator handles addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division. A scientific calculator goes much further:

- **Trigonometric functions** — sin, cos, tan, and their inverses (arcsin, arccos, arctan)
- **Logarithms** — log base 10 and natural log (ln)
- **Exponents and roots** — powers, square roots, cube roots, nth roots
- **Factorials** — n! calculations
- **Constants** — π (pi) and e (Euler's number)
- **Parentheses** — for controlling order of operations in complex expressions
- **Memory functions** — store and recall values mid-calculation

## Key Functions Explained

### Trigonometric Functions

| Function | What It Does | Example |
|---|---|---|
| sin | Sine of an angle | sin(30°) = 0.5 |
| cos | Cosine of an angle | cos(60°) = 0.5 |
| tan | Tangent of an angle | tan(45°) = 1 |
| sin⁻¹ | Inverse sine (arcsin) | sin⁻¹(0.5) = 30° |
| cos⁻¹ | Inverse cosine (arccos) | cos⁻¹(0.5) = 60° |
| tan⁻¹ | Inverse tangent (arctan) | tan⁻¹(1) = 45° |

Make sure to switch between **degrees** and **radians** depending on what your calculation requires. Most high school math uses degrees; calculus and physics often use radians.

### Logarithms

- **log** — Logarithm base 10 (common log). Used in chemistry (pH), acoustics (decibels), and engineering.
- **ln** — Natural logarithm (base e). Used in calculus, finance (compound growth), and physics.
- **10^x** — The inverse of log. Raises 10 to the power of x.
- **e^x** — Raises Euler's number (≈2.71828) to the power of x.

### Exponents and Roots

- **x²** — Squares the current value
- **x³** — Cubes the current value
- **xʸ** — Raises x to any power y
- **√** — Square root
- **∛** — Cube root
- **ⁿ√x** — nth root of x

## Tips for Accurate Calculations

- **Check degree vs. radian mode** before doing any trig calculations. Wrong mode = wrong answer.
- **Use parentheses liberally.** When in doubt about order of operations, add parentheses to force the correct order.
- **Read the display carefully.** Scientific calculators show more decimal places than basic calculators. Results like `3.141592653` are precise — don't round them until the very end.
- **For very large or very small numbers**, the calculator uses scientific notation (e.g., `1.5 × 10⁸`). This is normal.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What's the difference between degrees and radians?**
Both measure angles, but using different units. A full circle is 360 degrees or 2π radians. Degrees are more intuitive for everyday use. Radians are used in calculus and physics because they simplify many formulas. Our [Scientific Calculator](/tools/scientific-calculator) lets you switch between both modes.

**Can I use the calculator from my keyboard?**
Yes. You can type numbers and operators directly from your keyboard. Press Enter or = to calculate. This makes it faster than clicking the on-screen buttons.

**Is the online scientific calculator as accurate as a physical one?**
Yes. The calculator uses standard JavaScript floating-point math, which is the same precision used by most scientific calculators and computing tools. For most calculations, you'll never notice a difference.

**Does it support order of operations (PEMDAS)?**
Yes. The [Scientific Calculator](/tools/scientific-calculator) correctly applies the standard order of operations: Parentheses, Exponents, Multiplication and Division (left to right), Addition and Subtraction (left to right). You can also use parentheses to control the order manually.
